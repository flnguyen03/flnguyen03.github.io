Google Data Analytics Capstone: Cyclistic Bike Share

October 27, 2022

Preface:

I’ll be presenting my data analysis to a big audience. Several people present are, Lily Moreno: the director of marketing and my manager over at a fictious company named, Cyclistic including the marketing analystics team and the executive team. For detail context of the capstone case study including steps of the data preparations, please follow this Github link.

Background: “How do annual members and casual riders use Cyclistic bikes differently?”.

We will take a look at a Power BI dashboard I develop using Calendar Year 2021 data, Cyclistic Bike Share Data. I choose to isolate my analysis to Calendar Year 2021 to observe a one-year trend initial as the Calendar Year 2022 hasn’t completed yet.

Presentation:

Opening remarks: Greetings director Ms. Moreno, folks in the marketing analystics team, and the executive team.

Slogan: “We aim to provide customers eco-friendly alternative access to transportation.”

(slide #0)

Slide #1: First slide, we will observe the different ride types our company offers and membership usage for the Calendar Year 2021.

(slide #1)

Observe that with the different “ride types”, casual riders consist ~45% versus ~55% annual membership riders.

Slide #2: Moving to the second slide, I will filter our “ride types” to focus mainly on electric and classic bikes when observing trends between casual versus member riders.

(slide #2)

  • Notice that member riders have a greater number of rides compared to casual riders (top-right visual)
  • In contrast, casual riders spent more time on bikes than member riders (bottom-left visual)
  • Member and casual riders have their peak usage of our service during the summer months June to September
  • Members have their peak usage during the early morning (morning work rush hour) and early evening (evening work rush hour)
  • Causal riders have continuous increase of usage from sunrise to peaking near sunset time

(slide #2.2)

  • Casual riders use Cyclistic bikes more on Saturday and Sunday than during the weekdays
  • Annual members constantly ride daliy while teetering usage down on Sunday

Slide #3: Next slide, I’ve broken out the monthly aggregate average of ride distance (meters) and ride time (seconds) of each rider type.

(slide #3)

Across the months, the average ride distance do not vary significantly in the rider types. Though the average ride time do significantly vary between the casual and member riders, at most double the average ride time. Casual riders are leisurely using the bicycles versus members utilizing the bicycles to get to their location quickly.

Slide #4: The following slide, I adjust our focus to the summer months to observe any aggregate average trends.

(slide #4)

Members are spending half the average time on the bicycles compared to the causal riders. Interestly, I see that the average ride time decrease for the casual riders during the summer than other months.

Slide #5: Slide 5, I look at some station usage to see where casual and members are riding bicycles.

(slide #5)

(slide #6)

The top 10 Stations are close to popular landmarks and near the Chicago waterfront.

Slide #7: Last slide, I further investigate the bottom list station usage among the casual and member riders.

(slide #7)

The bottom ten stations are further away from the Chicago waterfront and popular landmarks.

Conclusion:

Recommendations

  1. Marketing team should advertise incentives of annual membership passes for casual riders beginning of the summer, ideally during the months of April or May.

  2. Target higher 11-25% discounts in the bottom station locations to encourage casual riders in those area to get annual memberships.

  3. Alternatively, target higher 11-25% discounts in the top station locations to encourage casual riders in those areas to be encouraged to convert to an annual membership.
